Joy Harjo: A Quick Overview

  • Born: 1951, Tulsa, OK
  • Her troubled childhood had a huge influence on her art in adulthood
  • 23rd Poet Laureate of the United States from 2019-2022
  • Second ever Poet Laureate to be appointed for three terms under that title
  • Creek/Mvskoke Nation heritage
  • Has published several children's books and released 7 award-winning music albums
  • Aside from poetry and music, she is well-known as a champion of Native and Women's rights

Poetry

Harjo's poetry is ladden with vivid nature imagery, influenced by her Mvskoke Creek heritage. In many of her poems, subtle references to Mvskoke folklore make an appearance, adding to the rich discussion on love, trauma, growth, resistance, and spirituality. Harjo's personal experiences trickle into her writing, and her poems tend to be very open and raw and vulnerable.

Her writing is incredibly intentional. My favorite example of this is her poem "Becoming Seventy," in which she crafts exactly 70 stanzas with each stanza steadily growing longer. Her poems typically do not fit a particular rhyme scheme or structure. However, the structure of each poem is very carefully constructed in cooperation with its message. In her poem titled "Insomnia and the Seven Steps to Grace," the stanzas follow an alternating pattern. One is long, the next is short, the next is long again. The structure mimics the lull of a sleep-deprived mind. On the other hand, "Invisible Fish" is written all in one stanza with short, punctuated sentences, mostly void of description until the very last sentence. The poem itself is about the cyclical nature of the destruction and the way we change the Earth and ourselves for our benefit eventually means the world leaves us and our past behind. The poem's shortness and dry descriptions of "earlier" events reinforces the fleeting, forgetable nature of the past. The long, descriptive sentence at the end punctuates how much has changed and how little people have noticed.

Though Harjo's poetry is filled with references to nature, her poems mostly center around the human experience. This means that most of her poems end up exploring the deep connection between humans and the nature around them. In many of the poems, the Earth functions as a sentient character whom the narrator often interacts with. A particularly strong theme across her writing is the raw, unadulterated view on the Indigenous experience. Her poems in and of themselves are a form of resistance and preservation of Mvskoke history. As a poet and an activist, she speaks not only for the Mvskoke Nation, but for Indigenous peoples all over the country.

Poem Analysis

It was way back, before there was a way back
When time threaded earth and sky.
Children were conceived, were born, grew, and walked tall
In what we now call a day.
There must have been two suns, a bright moon, somehow
We had more light than now, sheen
Of falling in love playing about Earth’s body
In a wild flicker which lit
Us up. We who were this planet and yearned for touch.
Every planted thought grew plant
Ladders to the stars, way back, before there was
No way back, Miss Mary Mack.
We used to sing along the buttons of her
Dress. Our babies are always
Our babies. Even back then when time waved through
The corn. We knew our plants like
Relatives. Their stories were our stories, there
Were songs for everything — I
Should say “are” songs for every transformation
They link between way back and
Now, the forever now, a time when a young
Mvskoke man and woman
Walked through the shimmer of the early evening.
They had become as one song.
They lay down when it was dark. I can hear their
Intimate low-voice talking.
How they tease one another with such gut love.
Earth makes a bed, with pillow
Mounds. And it is there as the night insects sing
They conceived their first child. They
Will look back as they walk East toward the sunrise.
The raw stalks of beginning
Will drink the light, root deeply dark into earth.
In the tracks of their loving
The plant-child emerges, first the seed head, then
Leafy, long male body and the white female
Flowers of tobacco, or
Hece, as the people called it when it called
To them. Come here. We were brought
To you from those who love you. We will help you.
And that’s how it began, way
Back, when we knew how to hear the songs of plants
And could sing back, like now
On paper, with marks like bird feet, but where are
Our ears? They have grown to fit
Around earbuds, to hear music made for cold
Cash, like our beloved smoke-
Making threaded with addiction and dead words.
Sing this song back to me, girl.
In the moonlight, tobacco plant had silver
Moon buttons all up her back.
We’re getting dressed to go plant new songs with words.
Our sun is dimming faster.
Mvto hece, mvto hvse, mvto e — 
Kanvchaga, mvto ah

1. In many of her interviews, Harjo refers to her poems informally as "___ poem" or "___ story." This poem specifically focuses on telling the tale of the Mvskoke Creek Nation's creation of the tobacco plant. It comes from her larger collection titled An American Sunrise that explores the history of her ancestors and the stories they left behind. These lines make it very clear that Harjo intends to tell us a story. The idea of time "threading" earth and sky makes me think she refers to a time when day light and moon light were the only sources of light. Yet it also does the work of making the scenery feel so connected and complete. Everything is sewn together. Everything is in place.

2. Children grew up quickly. I think it's significant that while it's clear that she's saying the children grew quickly, she doesn't mention maturity anywhere. As though it wasn't a consideration at that time. As though the children were never immature. While it seems like such a simple line it really does the most work of showing us just how different times were then to how they are now.

3. She's using the light as a symbol for life. We were more alive back then. The line also seems to suggest the moon is no longer bright. Put in context of the poem which actively resists the colonization for profit of the tobacco plant and the effect that's had especially on night life, it feels like a clever way to say that the nights have gotten more dangerous and depressing; dimmer, shrouded in a darker darkness. I love that Harjo explains the presence of light as "falling in love playing about the Earth's body." It makes that light seem all the more warm, maybe to contrast it to light we have today which just exists.

4. And then she equates mankind to the Earth that "falling in love" gently wraps around with the falling rays of the sun. Like in many of her other poems, Harjo emphasizes the human connection with the Earth. But I think the key detail here is that the way the speaker is using the past tense. We used to be the Earth. We used to yearn for touch, but all that has been lost with time. Moving the "us up" down to these lines insted of finishing the sentence in the previous section forces the reader to pause and really notice that Harjo was intentional about the effect being on us.

5. Like in several of her other poems, Harjo makes a reference to the stars. In Mvskoke Creek culture and folklore, the stars are seen as spiritual symbols, often representative of ancestors. In these lines, Harjo seems to mean that each thought had some connection to ancient tradition or with the ancestors in mind. Of course, only before there was "No way back."

6. No way back, Miss Mary Mack. This is an interesting blend of traditional ancient folklore and the stuff that she (and most of her audience) probably grew up with as kids. The rhyme "Miss Mary Mack" is often interpreted as the whimsical story of a girl who actively grows up, in a strange way gaining independence from her parents and perhaps jumping over the fence with the elephants that seem to represent a larger state of being. Harjo uses this to build these lines that seem to say we've moved into a point of no return. In the modern day, we've jumped over the fence, and we've stopped planting thoughts the way they used to, and we may never be able to again.

7. Harjo writes about babies and familial possession and the plants all in the same breath. The plants are family. She brings up their stories, and it has the effect of showing us their relationship with the wildlife in a much more intimate way than just saying they could communicate with the plants. This is an important building factor that explains why she feels such strong attachment to the tobacco plant and why its loss affects her acutely. To a people whose plants are their babies, its like ripping the baby from their arms and selling it.

8. The first word in this line is "Were." It's a little odd that she places that much emphasis on the word when she corrects it later in the poem anyway. But in the very next line, she starts it with "Should," and that feels like she's saying that even though she should, she won't because the essence of these songs has been lost, maybe even stolen.

9. Harjo emphasizes this elusive "forever now." She's saying that this link can never be broken. As long as time passes, the past will always be connected to the present with this link. Then the story of the creation of the tobacco plant really begins. And it starts with Mvskoke people. "The shimmer of early evening" draws a very intimate and serene image.

10. These lines stand primarily to depict the love between these two individuals. The birthing of tobacco was not a faceless emotionless event. It was born of gentle, teasing, and tender love. And like everything else in this poem, "gut love" is an incredibly rich and raw phrase. To love with all your gut is to love from the deepest, most vulnerable part of your soul.

11. It almost feels too intimate to be reading. Harjo builds this moment to equate the planting of the seed as the conceiving of the baby. The tobacco plant is as her ancestor and her sister. The way she integrated the bed and the night time really makes it seem like the plant was conceived the same way a human baby is, but the details that follow make that distinction much more clear.

12. The symbolism of the parents walking towards the sunrise is worth exploring. They walk towards a new horizon, potentially towards the end of their own time and the birth of a new generation. Yet they look back towards the tobacco plant to see it fighting for life and thriving. That is the their blood's work. That is the proliferation of their being. That is their new start.

13. And finally the tobacco plant grows. The flower opens its petals to the gentle loving of the light and the roots draw up the gentle loving of the Earth. The Earth is the people. Nowadays, with the way it's been commercialized, tobacco is mostly known as the powder wrapped in a cigarette. Harjo depicts its flower. And it is the result of the man and the woman with the leafy beautiful stalk and the white flower, white like a blank canvas.

14. While I couldn't find a direct translation for "Hece," I know its a Mvskoke word, since at the end of the poem, its paired with "Mvto." While the language and the name is infinitely important, the focus of these lines was elsewhere. All these lines come together to show the deep relationship between the tobacco plant and the Mvskoke. It's a bond that crosses generations. They see themselves as the guardians of the tobacco, the ones nurturing their loved one's beloved in their absence. It goes back to the first few lines where Harjo talked about the thoughts that were planted and grew all the way to the stars. Their actions were taken with their ancestors constantly in the forefront of their minds. The love and the connection and the generational bridges in these lines add a layer of emotional complexity to the poem.

15. It's very intentional that Harjo mentions being able to hear the plants before she mentions being able to sing back to them. In the first place, the lines before these had hinted that the people from back then had offered themselves in service to the plant. Naturally, the listening comes before the speech. It puzzles me a little why she includes the "like now" here instead of on the next line. Her explicit mentioning of "way back" makes it clear that it is not like now. I suppose that has the effect of making the reader furrow their brow and look again at the words and work to hear the meaning.

16. This is definitely my favorite set of lines in the poem. One of my favorite things about An American Sunrise is that its message is so intimately interwoven with the resistance. Harjo does not write to entertain. Harjo does not write to settle. She writes to provoke, she writes to change, she writes to rewrite. And all of that is so beautifully embodied in this poem where she defends the tobacco stolen from her people and exploited for its addictiveness. Tobacco is not criminal. It is love and heritage made criminal by the man with money. She starts these lines continuing the sentence fragment, comparing singing to writing. It expresses her frustration at being able to sing back, but not being able to listen. But she mentions that our ears are wrapped around earbuds. They are not gone, they are occupied by the moneymen. They, like the tobacco, have been stolen. But still, they are not gone. The resistance, suppressed, lives.

17. Now it seems to be the tobacco plant speaking, inviting the narrator (perhaps Harjo, herself) to sing back. This seems to be a turning point where the narrator can finally hear the song of the tobacco plant. And now, the Miss Mary Mack allusion comes back, but this time it is dressed around the tobacco plant. Silver buttons, perhaps symbolizing maturity, now adorn the plant, fastened to the dress with the light of the moon. From the sun rise to the high of the moon, the tobacco plant has grown and matured. Now, like its mother and father, the sunrise awaits on the other side of the moon light.

18. The ending starts at these lines. It feels like a cascade and a relooping of the story. We assume the moon has fallen and the tobacco plant has left into the sun rise with its ancestors. The song of life and origin will play itself in a new mound of earth as the people prepare. The last part we are meant to understand tells us the sun is dimming faster. It seems ominous, like it could mean there is less and less time for the plants and the people to grow or that the stories following this one will begin to taper, but it could also mean that the resistance is picking up. That the stories are growing faster and faster and that the people can hear again.

19. I love this detail. The language is so important to the poem because the origin story of the Mvskoke tobacco is not meant to be delivered in only English. According to a quick Google search, the first line is composed of three variations of gratitude-expession. I could not find what "Kanvchaga" meant. "Mvcho" again means "thank you." Harjo leaves it up to the reader to determine who is being thanked. I like to imagine its the ancestors that she wrote about much earlier. That right now, the narrator is planting the seeds and as it was done before, the seeds are being planted with the eye trained on the ancestors, with the wishes that the seeds will grow to the stars.
